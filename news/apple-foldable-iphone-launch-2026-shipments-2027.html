Analyst Says Apple’s Foldable iPhone Could Launch in 2026 But Widespread Shipments May Not Hit Until 2027

December 19, 2025
By: Shedrack

Key Analyst Warning

TF International Securities analyst Ming-Chi Kuo has issued a significant warning about Apple's anticipated entry into the foldable smartphone market. In a new research note, Kuo states that while Apple is still expected to announce its first foldable iPhone in the second half of 2026, "smooth shipments may not occur until 2027" due to substantial production challenges.

This suggests that even if Apple showcases the device next year, consumers might face extended shortages and limited availability well into the following year.

The Long-Awaited Foldable iPhone Faces Manufacturing Hurdles

Apple's rumored foldable iPhone, often referred to as the "iPhone Fold," represents one of the most anticipated product launches in the company's history. However, according to the latest analysis from renowned Apple supply chain expert Ming-Chi Kuo, the road to market may be longer than enthusiasts hope. Despite progress, the development is reportedly behind earlier expectations.

The core issue lies in achieving manufacturability at scale. Kuo specifically cites "early-stage yield and ramp-up challenges" that are expected to constrain supply. This means that even after a public announcement, Apple may struggle to produce enough units to meet what is anticipated to be very strong initial demand.

Understanding the Delay: Challenges and Implications

Kuo's warning highlights the significant technical hurdles involved in creating a reliable, mass-producible foldable device that meets Apple's stringent quality standards. Other analysts, like those at Mizuho Securities, have echoed similar concerns, suggesting that unresolved design elements like the hinge mechanism could even push the full launch to 2027.

Core Production Challenges

Yield Issues

Early-stage manufacturing yields for the complex display and hinge assemblies are reportedly lower than needed for mass production, limiting output.

Ramp-Up Difficulties

Scaling production from pilot lines to the massive volumes required for a global iPhone launch is proving challenging.

Supply Chain Complexity

The device relies on a "complex mix of specialty glass and materials" from suppliers like Corning and SCHOTT, creating a intricate supply chain.

While Apple remains secretive, numerous leaks and analyst reports have painted a picture of what the first foldable iPhone might entail. The device is expected to be a premium, book-style foldable that prioritizes a seamless user experience.

Market Context: Apple's Late Entry & The Foldable Landscape

If Apple launches in 2026-2027, it will be entering a foldable market that competitors like Samsung, Google, and various Chinese OEMs have cultivated for nearly a decade. Apple's stated goal is reportedly to solve persistent user experience issues, most notably the visible crease in the display, with a "complex" hinge design.

Industry projections suggest Apple's entry could be a major catalyst. Foldable panel shipments are forecast to grow 46% in 2026, with Apple potentially capturing over 22% of the global foldable smartphone market in its first year. However, this ambitious projection is contingent on Apple solving the very production problems Kuo has highlighted.

What This Means for Consumers & the Industry

Ming-Chi Kuo's analysis suggests a cautious approach for consumers eagerly awaiting a foldable iPhone. The pattern may resemble the launch of the original iPhone X, which was announced on schedule but shipped in very limited quantities initially. Potential buyers should be prepared for the possibility of a premium-priced device that is extremely hard to purchase at launch.

For Apple, the situation underscores the immense difficulty of innovating in a new form factor while maintaining its reputation for quality and reliability. The delay in achieving smooth shipments indicates that the company is prioritizing getting the product right over rushing it to market. The success of this strategy will determine whether the foldable iPhone becomes a transformative hit or remains a niche, low-volume product in its first generation.
